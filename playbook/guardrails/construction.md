# Construction Guardrails

## Subcontractors

### The cheapest bid is the most expensive
**The rule:** Lowest price almost always means: slower, more change orders, worse quality, 
or they misread the scope. Vet based on reliability first, price second. 
A sub who shows up on time and builds to spec at a fair price is worth 15% more than a gamble.

### Three warnings and you're out
**The rule:** Document every sub performance issue. After three documented issues 
(missed dates, quality failures, safety violations), they're off the preferred list. 
Don't give a fourth chance just because switching is inconvenient.

### Never start without a signed contract
**The rule:** No verbal starts. No "we'll figure out the contract later." 
Sub must have signed scope, price, and schedule before mobilizing. 
This protects both sides and prevents every scope dispute.

## Schedule

### Weather is not a surprise
**The rule:** In Chicago, assume you lose 4–6 weeks between November and March. 
Build it into the schedule upfront. "Weather delay" is not an excuse — it's a planning failure.

### Concrete and steel lead times will surprise you
**The rule:** Get structural material quotes and lead times *during DD*, not after CDs. 
A 12-week lead time discovered at permit = 3 months of dead time.

### The last 10% takes 30% of the time
**The rule:** Punch list, final inspections, and closeout always take longer than scheduled. 
Budget 4–6 weeks of finish/closeout time. Don't promise occupancy based on drywall completion.

## Site Conditions

### Always get a geotech
**The rule:** Never skip the geotechnical report. Even on a site that "looks fine." 
Bad soil = foundation redesign = $50K+ and 6 weeks you didn't plan for.

### Assume underground surprises
**The rule:** In Chicago, especially on tear-down lots, budget for: abandoned foundations, 
old fuel tanks, utility conflicts, water table issues. If the demo budget doesn't include contingency, it's wrong.
